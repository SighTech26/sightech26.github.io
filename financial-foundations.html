Module 1

Budgeting

A budget is a plan for your money — it tells your money where to go instead of wondering where it went. The 50/30/20 rule is one of the simplest frameworks to get started.

The 50/30/20 Rule

50% Needs — essentials like food, transport, your phone plan.
30% Wants — entertainment, dining out, clothing, hobbies.
20% Savings — money you set aside to grow and protect your future.

Needs vs. wants

The most important budgeting skill isn't maths — it's honest self-awareness. Before you spend, ask: is this something I truly need, or something I want?

Needs are non-negotiables — food, transport to school or work, essential utilities. Without them, your daily life is seriously impacted.

Wants are extras that make life enjoyable but are optional. There's nothing wrong with wants — the key is funding needs and savings first, then enjoying what's left.

Budgeting isn't about deprivation. It's about being intentional — so that when you do spend on wants, you're choosing to, not just drifting.

Module 2

Compound Interest

When your savings earn interest, that interest earns its own interest — creating a snowball effect that grows faster the longer it runs. Einstein reportedly called it the "eighth wonder of the world."

The formula: A = P × (1 + r/n)^(n×t) + future value of monthly contributions

You don't need to memorise this — the calculator below handles the maths. The key idea: time is your most valuable input.

Why starting early matters so much

If Emma saves $100/month from age 16 at 6% annual return, she'll have around $200,000 by age 65. If Jake starts the same habit at age 26 — just 10 years later — he'll have roughly $100,000. Same effort, half the outcome.

The difference is the time compound interest had to work. Those extra 10 years give interest time to compound on itself exponentially.

It works the other way too. Credit card debt at 20% interest grows the same way against you. Understanding this dynamic is just as important as knowing how to grow savings.

Module 3

Savings Goals

A savings goal turns a wish into a plan. Set a specific target, a deadline, and a monthly amount — giving your money a clear purpose.

The SMART Framework: Specific · Measurable · Achievable · Relevant · Time-bound

Example: "Save $800 for a laptop in 8 months" is a SMART goal. "Save more money" is not.

Three habits that actually work

Pay yourself first

When you get paid, set aside your savings amount immediately — before spending on anything else. This removes the temptation to spend first and save whatever's left.

Build an emergency fund first

Before saving for fun goals, aim to build a small cushion of $200–$500. This protects you from unexpected costs without needing to go into debt.

Track your spending for one month

Log every purchase for 30 days. Most people are surprised how much small, frequent purchases — coffees, snacks, subscriptions — add up over a month.
